# Nepal's Anti-Corruption Landscape

> *References: Corruption in Nepal — A Landscape Analysis. Sources: Transparency International (2024), Klitgaard (1991), Subedi (2024), UNODC (2018), Prevention of Corruption Act 2059 (2002), Constitution of Nepal (2015), CIAA Act (1991).*

## Introduction

Corruption is broadly recognized as the "abuse of entrusted power for private gain" (Transparency International, 2024). In the Nepalese context, the legal framework defines corruption primarily through the Prevention of Corruption Act, 2059 (2002), which identifies it as a range of punishable offenses committed by public servants or those influencing them (Section 2, Prevention of Corruption Act, 2002). Academically, it is often modeled as a function of monopoly and discretion minus accountability, where systemic weaknesses allow public officials to deviate from formal duties for personal, familial, or group pecuniary gain (Klitgaard, 1991; Subedi, 2024).

The Constitution of Nepal (2015) primarily empowers the Commission for the Investigation of Abuse of Authority (CIAA) as the apex constitutional body to investigate and prosecute acts of corruption (Article 239, Constitution of Nepal). Under current law, corruption in Nepal is categorized into several distinct forms:

### Bribery and Graft
The act of accepting or agreeing to accept any benefit, cash, or goods to perform or forbear an official duty (Section 3, Prevention of Corruption Act, 2002).

### Abuse of Authority
The misuse of official position with dishonest or mala-fide intention to obtain illegal benefits or cause loss to the government (CIAA Act, 1991).

### Illicit Enrichment
The acquisition of property or assets by a public servant that is significantly disproportionate to their known source of legal income (Section 20, Prevention of Corruption Act, 2002).

### Revenue Leakage
Actions by public officials that result in the loss of state revenue through embezzlement or assisted tax evasion (Section 7, Prevention of Corruption Act, 2002).

### Fraud and Falsification
The preparation of false documents, reports, or translations with the intent to deceive the state or secure illegal advantages.

While Nepal has ratified the United Nations Convention Against Corruption (UNCAC), the domestic legal scope is currently focused on the public sector, with ongoing legislative debates regarding the extension of CIAA jurisdiction to the private sector and non-governmental organizations (UNODC, 2018; Kathmandu Post, 2015).

## Investigating Authority

### Commission for the Investigation of Abuse of Authority (CIAA) (अख्तियार दुरुप्रयोग अनुसन्धान आयोग)

CIAA is a constitutional body for controlling corruption by investigating any abuse of authority committed through corruption by any person holding public office. The provisions of Articles 97 and 98 of the then Constitution of the Kingdom of Nepal (1990) had granted CIAA the status of an independent and autonomous constitutional body.

The CIAA operates under a comprehensive legal framework that includes:

- **The Commission for the Investigation of Abuse of Authority Act, 1991**: Provides for the creation and operational guidelines for the CIAA.
- **The Prevention of Corruption Act, 2002**: Strengthens the CIAA's authority to fight corruption in public offices.
- **The Commission for the Investigation of Abuse of Authority Rules, 2002**: Establishes the operational procedures and internal governance of the CIAA.

These laws, alongside the CIAA's own procedures, directives, and guidelines, allow it to work with principles of integrity, fairness, and fearlessness.

#### Administrative Support

According to Article 21a of the Commission for the Investigation of Abuse of Authority Act, 1991, the Government of Nepal is required to provide personnel and resources as per the approved posts to support the operations of the CIAA. Personnel from the civil service, Nepal Police, and other government services are seconded to the Commission, enabling it to perform its duties effectively.

In summary, the CIAA, through its well-established legal framework and multi-faceted approach, plays a crucial role in fighting corruption and promoting good governance in Nepal.

## Commission Structure

There shall be CIAA, consisting of chief commissioner and four other commissioners. The Chief Commissioner acts as a chairperson of the CIAA.

The President shall, on recommendation of the Constitutional Council, appoint the Chief Commissioner and Commissioners.

The term of office of the Chief Commissioner and Commissioners shall be six years from the date of appointment.

Notwithstanding anything contained in clause (3), the office of the Chief Commissioner or a Commissioner shall be vacant in any of the following circumstances:

- if he or she tenders resignation in writing to the President,
- if he or she attains the age of sixty-five years,
- if a motion of impeachment is passed against him or her under Article 101,
- if he or she is removed from office by the President on recommendation of the Constitutional Council on grounds of his or her inability to hold office and discharge the functions due to physical or mental illness,
- if he or she dies.

The Chief Commissioner and Commissioners are not eligible for reappointment. However, a Commissioner can be appointed as the Chief Commissioner, and the term will include their time as Commissioner.

A person shall be eligible to be appointed as the Chief Commissioner or a Commissioner of the Commission for the Investigation of Abuse of Authority if he or she possesses the following qualification:

- A bachelor's degree from a recognized university.
- Not being affiliated with any political party at the time of appointment.
- At least 20 years of experience in fields such as accounting, revenue, law, development, or research.
- At least 45 years old.
- Possession of high moral character.

The remuneration and other conditions of service of the Chief Commissioner and the Commissioners shall be as provided for in the Federal law. The remuneration and conditions of service of the Chief Commissioner and the Commissioners shall not, so long as they hold office, be altered to their disadvantage.

Provided that this provision shall not apply in cases where a state of emergency is declared because of extreme economic disarrays.

A person once appointed as the Chief Commissioner or Commissioner shall not be eligible for appointment in other government service.

Provided that, they may be eligible for appointments in political positions, or in positions related to investigations, research, or advisory roles.
